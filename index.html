The Situation

Scotland's condition

Scotland is not a colony, but it is not an equal partner either. It is a semi-peripheral territory within a core imperial state — one whose surplus has been systematically extracted for generations, whose industrial communities were deliberately destroyed, and whose working class continues to pay the price of decisions made in its name but not in its interest.

The North Sea oil windfall generated £52.4 billion in tax revenues in six years after 1979 alone. It was used to sustain an overvalued pound and finance the destruction of manufacturing — not to rebuild the communities whose labour had powered the imperial economy. The defeat of the miners in 1984–85 broke not just a union but a class formation.

The geography of Scotland's drug deaths maps onto the geography of deindustrialisation with the precision of a structural law.

The 2008 financial crisis was not a banking accident but the return of a profitability crisis that financialisation had displaced without resolving. The austerity that followed was a political choice — to make workers pay for a crisis generated by finance capital. Scotland entered that conjuncture with the full weight of deindustrialisation bearing down on a working class that had never fully recovered from the defeats of the 1980s.

The class forces

Scotland retains one of the most concentrated patterns of land ownership in the developed world. Fewer than five hundred individuals own more than half of Scotland's private land — not a feudal survival but a functioning structure of capitalist accumulation that generates rents, captures subsidies, and exercises disproportionate political power.

The Scottish working class is defined by the extraordinary proportion employed in the state sector, by the growth of a new urban proletariat in hospitality, care, and the gig economy, and by the systematic exclusion of the most exploited workers from the organised labour movement. Union density in the Scottish private sector has collapsed to historically low levels.

The decisive mechanism of working-class discipline in contemporary Scotland is debt. The financialisation of housing has bound large sections of the working class to mortgage or rent obligations that suppress the capacity to withdraw labour, change employers, or engage in any form of economic risk-taking. It is the privatised incomes policy of the austerity era.

Scotland's health disparities are among the most extreme in Western Europe — class exploitation written directly onto human bodies.
01

Deindustrialisation

The deliberate destruction of Scotland's industrial base concentrated costs on working-class communities least able to bear them. The drug deaths crisis, the health gap, the atomisation of community life — all trace back to this.

02

Land monopoly

The Highland Clearances were the foundational act of Scottish primitive accumulation. That process of dispossession has never been reversed. The communities cleared have not returned. The land has not been restored.

03

Energy extraction

Scotland possesses among the best renewable energy resources in Europe. The profits flow outward to shareholders in London, New York, and beyond. Scottish consumers pay among the highest energy prices in Europe.

04

Political management

Devolution was designed to absorb the demand for self-determination without threatening the structures of British capitalist power. The SNP channelled post-2008 radicalisation into a constitutional question, leaving the class question intact.

Capital, Empire and Decline

Our foundational theoretical text. An analysis of Scotland's position within British imperialism, the structural decline of the British state, and the specific conditions this creates for national and socialist transformation. Available now in serialised form via Scottish Vanguard.

The Scottish Question and the Road to Power

Our strategic document. Sets out the analysis of the national question in Scotland, the relationship between independence and socialist transformation, and the organisational strategy through which working-class power is to be built. Publication forthcoming.

Strategy

The road to power

The principal contradiction in Scotland is between the Scottish proletariat and the capitalist-imperialist bloc composed of the Scottish bourgeoisie, the British state, and international monopoly capital. This cannot be resolved within the bounds of the United Kingdom or through parliamentary reform alone. The Party therefore adopts a two-stage revolutionary strategy.

Stage one

The National-Democratic Revolution

Led by the working class in alliance with the rural poor, progressive intellectuals, and patriotic elements of the petite bourgeoisie.

  • Proclaim the Scottish Republic
  • End British domination
  • Nationalise land and abolish feudal estates
  • Nationalise the banks and create a Scottish Central Bank
  • Establish a sovereign Scottish currency
  • Nationalise the commanding heights of the economy
  • Guarantee balanced regional development
  • Rebuild Scottish culture, language, and science
Stage two

The Socialist Revolution

The working class, organised and led by the Party, seizes full state power and establishes the Workers' Republic of Scotland.

  • Establish workers' control of production
  • Rational nationalisation of the economy
  • Implement the First Five-Year Plan
  • Nationalise all housing and abolish landlordism
  • Socialise care, domestic labour, and community reproduction
  • Reconstruct agriculture through state cooperatives
  • Expand scientific and polytechnical education
  • Repudiate unjust debts
Theory of transition

Neither electoralism nor insurrection

The Party rejects both the parliamentary road and the insurrectionary road as currently applicable in Scotland. The working class has not yet developed the organisational density or institutional forms of dual power that would make either viable or stable.

The revolutionary road in Scotland passes through the construction of mass working-class power — organisational, economic, and political — in the period before the revolutionary crisis, and through the capacity to act decisively when that crisis matures.

Dual power

The strategic concept guiding the Party's theory of transition is dual power: the construction, alongside and in opposition to the existing state, of working-class institutions capable of performing the functions of governance — economic coordination, social provision, political representation, security.

The class that has built institutions of its own — that has demonstrated its capacity to run the economy and maintain social order — is a class prepared to exercise state power. The class that has not built these institutions will find the revolutionary moment passes before it is ready.

Membership

Get involved

We are not looking for enthusiasm. We are looking for people who demonstrate consistency over time — who attend, contribute, follow through, and are capable of sustained political work. The conversation will be direct, unhurried, and without pressure.

Entry point
Join the Scottish Workers Network. Attend a reading group or event. Get to know the organisation through practical participation before any further commitment is discussed.
The conversation
After initial engagement, a one-to-one political conversation. We are assessing fit as much as you are. No recruitment pressure. Political alignment clarifies through engagement, not in advance of it.
What we offer
Political education, collective analysis, practical organising experience, and a serious organisation committed to long-term development of a real working-class force in Scotland.
What we ask
Reliability. Consistency. The capacity to sustain political work over time alongside real-life commitments. We build slowly and durably — not through sudden conversions.
